Greeley Accountability Coalition · 2026 · Cascadia/Catalyst Project
The Issues. The Demands.
Why This Matters.
The Cascadia/Catalyst Data Center project is one of the largest public-private development decisions in Greeley's history. It will affect your water supply, your taxes, your housing costs, and your neighbors' jobs — for generations. This page explains what we are demanding, why we are demanding it, and why citizen oversight is not optional.
⚠ Citizen Oversight Is Non-Negotiable
Council members have, in their own words, acknowledged a prior failure of transparency in how development decisions have been handled. They have conceded that the public has not been adequately informed or included in negotiations involving public resources. This concession is not a minor procedural note — it is a foundational admission.

Given that acknowledgment, it would be civic malpractice to respond by returning that same unchecked responsibility to the same individuals who failed to exercise it properly. Good intentions are not a substitute for structural accountability. History — in Greeley and in cities across America — shows that without independent oversight, development deals that promise public benefit routinely deliver private gain instead.

The Citizens Oversight Council is not a request. It is a condition. Every other demand on this scorecard depends on it. Without oversight, there is no enforcement. Without enforcement, promises are just words. The community did not come to the table asking for trust. We came asking for verification — and that verification requires a body of residents with real authority, named in writing, accepted by the council, and empowered to operate without a council veto.

Local Jobs & Economic Opportunity
Greeley First · Living Wages · Local Contracting
Development supported by public resources must produce measurable economic opportunity for the people of Greeley — not just jobs that go to out-of-town contractors and imported labor. We are demanding binding commitments, not voluntary pledges that disappear after the ribbon cutting.
  • Minimum 50% local hiring target for all construction and operational jobs tied to the project
  • Living wage standards with benefits for all project-related employment — no minimum-wage workarounds
  • State-registered apprenticeship programs prioritizing Greeley youth, workforce development participants, and previously incarcerated residents
  • At least 20% of procurement spending directed to Greeley-based businesses
  • Affordable commercial space allocated for Greeley startups, artists, inventors, and small businesses
3
Taxpayer Protection & Fiscal Transparency
No Hidden Deals · Full Public Accounting · On-Record Votes
Residents have a right to know exactly what their tax dollars are subsidizing. No backroom deals. No private agreements on public money. Every commitment involving public funds must be approved through a recorded City Council vote and made available for public review.
  • Full public disclosure of all subsidies, tax abatements, infrastructure investments, and public financing arrangements
  • All financial commitments approved through recorded, public City Council votes
  • Increased infrastructure investment for police, roads, water systems, and sewer maintenance impacted by development
  • Consideration of increasing the special retail tax at the arena from 3% to 12%, with funds directed to a public trust for community investment
  • No private agreements involving public funds — period
4
Water Stewardship & Environmental Responsibility
Independent Study · Developer Pays · Public Meetings
A data center of this scale has enormous water demands. Greeley's water is a shared resource and a long-term strategic asset for the entire region. No approval should move forward until the full impact is independently studied, publicly disclosed, and developer-funded mitigation is guaranteed in writing.
  • An independent water impact study by qualified third-party experts before any further project approvals
  • Full public release of study results and all associated data
  • Developer bears full mitigation costs for any environmental or water-resource impacts — not taxpayers
  • Developer funding for stormwater infrastructure and ongoing environmental monitoring
  • Regular public meetings to review environmental compliance, water usage, and air-quality impacts throughout the project lifecycle
5
Affordable Housing & Community Stability
50% Set-Aside · AMI-Tied Tiers · Eviction Prevention · Bus Hub · Homeless Investment
Large developments drive up housing costs in surrounding neighborhoods — and Cascadia will be no exception. Without binding deed restrictions written directly into the development agreement, affordable housing pledges are legally unenforceable and historically ignored. Greeley's Area Median Income is $68,650 annually. Every affordability calculation, every price threshold, and every income tier in this agreement must be anchored to that number — not to national averages, not to developer projections, not to aspirational targets.

Price ranges are guidance based on March 2026 30-year mortgage estimates. All binding commitments must reference AMI percentage thresholds, not fixed dollar amounts, to remain accurate as market conditions change.
  • 50% of all new housing within a 10-mile radius of Cascadia must be set aside for low- and moderate-income residents — binding, deed-restricted, and written into the development agreement. No voluntary pledges.
  • New housing must reflect the income levels of actual Greeley residents. All affordability definitions tied to Greeley AMI ($68,650) — not national or metro-wide figures.
  • Priority access for existing Greeley residents, including low-income households currently on housing waiting lists. First right of placement before any units are offered to outside buyers or renters.
  • Eviction prevention grants established for residents at risk of displacement due to Cascadia-driven rent and property value increases — specific dollar amounts must be quantified and committed in writing.
  • Property tax relief program for longtime Greeley homeowners facing property tax increases caused by Cascadia's impact on surrounding land values — dollar caps and eligibility thresholds to be publicly defined.
Transit — Bus Hub on Arena Land The City of Greeley owns land surrounding the arena. That land must be put to public use. We are demanding a full bus hub on that government-owned property — not a study, not a feasibility analysis, not a promise to "explore" transit. A committed, funded, operational bus hub with the following components:
  • Bus hub constructed on government-owned arena-adjacent land
  • New routes connecting the Cascadia/Catalyst site to neighboring cities — funded in coordination with those municipalities
  • Dedicated bus lanes near high-traffic Cascadia corridors
  • Fleet upgrades — updated, accessible buses on all new routes
  • Expanded stops throughout Greeley, with priority in low-income neighborhoods closest to the development
Homeless Services & Creative Funding The Cascadia project will generate significant new economic activity in Greeley. A portion of that activity must be directed toward the community members most at risk of being left behind. We are demanding:
  • Direct investment in Greeley homeless shelters — a specific dollar commitment, not a general pledge
  • Exploration of a dedicated sales tax mechanism to fund ongoing homeless services — with public vote and full transparency on fund usage
  • Developer contribution to a community stabilization fund as a condition of project approval

Accountability & Reporting Requirements
Annual Audits · Independent Verification · Public Data
Commitments without enforcement are promises without consequences. Every demand on this scorecard must be backed by binding reporting requirements.
  • Annual public reporting on all project outcomes
  • Independent audits of all development commitments
  • Ongoing oversight by the Citizens Oversight Council
  • Public access to all performance data related to the project
  • A temporary pause on major development approvals until public discussions have been completed
  • All negotiation sessions involving public funds recorded and publicly released within 24 hours
  • Written public response from each City Council member within 10 business days on whether they support this transparent process
